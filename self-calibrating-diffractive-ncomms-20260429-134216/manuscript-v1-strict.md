# Self-calibrating diffractive optical neural operators for imaging through dynamic aberrations

- Partially verified result classes: processor-level common-path gain under a shared ordinary-D2NN versus pilot-assisted-D2NN protocol.
- Unverified result classes: turbulence and thin phase-screen families under the same full protocol, strong modern electronic baselines that clearly exploit the pilot, fabrication-error robustness, and any experimental validation.
- Therefore this document is a strict manuscript draft and reviewer-facing scaffold, not a submission-ready paper.

## Abstract
Dynamic aberrations turn coherent image formation into a time-varying inverse problem whose state is often unknown at the moment of measurement. Existing optical correction strategies typically rely on active wavefront sensing, adaptive hardware, iterative optimization, or substantial electronic post-processing. Here we study a stricter question: whether a fixed diffractive optical processor can use a co-propagating optical reference to infer the instantaneous degradation state and improve image recovery without dynamic reconfiguration of the optics. Across Gaussian surrogate studies, wave-optics simulations with dynamic Zernike aberrations, information-theoretic and task-level analyses, and a phase-only passive-processor comparison tightened through structural wrong-reference redesign, the current evidence supports the bounded claim that a common-path pilot channel can reduce uncertainty about the present degradation and improve downstream recovery relative to no-reference and non-common-path controls. The same evidence also exposes the main unresolved issue: processor-level exclusion is improved but still not fully closed, with the current best confirmed structure showing a repeat-stable common-minus-ordinary gain and a positive risk-pair lower decile, yet still one small negative worst-case tail. The manuscript therefore advances a complete argument structure and evidence-bounded result package while leaving the final device-level claim open pending stronger processor-level robustness.

## Introduction
Dynamic aberrations, turbulence, scattering, defocus, and system drift remain central limits in microscopy, free-space imaging, and coherent computational imaging because they vary during acquisition rather than acting as one fixed blur kernel [1-8]. The practical difficulty is not only reduced resolution or contrast, but the fact that the degradation state itself changes from sample to sample and frame to frame, so image formation becomes a moving inverse problem [7-13]. Adaptive optics, wavefront shaping, and computational correction have each delivered powerful responses, yet they usually depend on explicit wavefront estimation, active modulation hardware, iterative feedback, or heavy digital reconstruction [2-4,9-13].

Diffractive optical neural networks and related free-space optical processors have expanded passive optical computing beyond static classification toward denoising, phase processing, analog inference, and inverse design [14-23]. Most such systems, however, are optimized for one forward model or for a training distribution that treats perturbations as nuisance variability rather than as an instantaneous latent state that can itself be inferred [14-21]. This leaves an unresolved question at the heart of passive optical intelligence: can a fixed diffractive processor become condition-aware without active retuning?

Existing routes do not fully close that gap. Robust training can average over a family of degradations, but it does not guarantee access to the specific realization acting on the present input. Guide-star and wavefront-sensing methods can estimate the aberration state, yet they often require dedicated sensing paths, active correction loops, or additional hardware [2-4,11,24-26]. Purely digital restoration can be powerful, but it shifts the burden to electronic inference and may fail out of distribution [12,13,27-36]. A physically cleaner route is to let a known reference field co-propagate with the object through the same unknown distortion, so that the recorded optical field already contains conditional information about the current degradation.

This manuscript develops that conditional-optics viewpoint for self-calibrating diffractive processing. Figure 1 introduces the common-path pilot concept and the control conditions used throughout the study. Figure 2 tests whether the effect survives a wave-optics forward model with dynamic Zernike aberrations. Figure 3 asks what can be said, within clear local limits, about the information carried by the pilot observation and the resulting task-level loss floor. Figure 4 checks whether the same mechanism transfers across downstream tasks. Figure 5 then presents the crucial processor-level comparison between ordinary and pilot-assisted passive diffractive networks. The present evidence supports the mechanism and its wave-optics persistence, and the current Figure 5 package now supports a more specific processor-level statement: a stable ordinary-versus-common-path advantage window exists in the lightweight passive protocol, but wrong-reference exclusion is presently best described by paired-distribution diagnostics rather than by mean PSNR alone. The paper is therefore written with strict evidence boundaries rather than submission-level certainty.

## Methods
### Optical formulation of common-path self-calibration
We consider an input optical field composed of an object-bearing channel and a known pilot channel that traverse the same unknown degradation operator before entering either a reconstruction rule or a passive diffractive processor. The common-path condition means that the pilot is exposed to the same instantaneous aberration realization as the object. The no-reference control removes the pilot entirely. The non-common-path control uses a pilot that does not share the same realization, and the wrong-reference control introduces a deliberately mismatched pilot. Because the detector records intensity, the combined observation carries auto- and cross-term information that can, in principle, constrain the latent degradation state. The verified claim in this manuscript is limited to the existence of useful conditional information under the current observation models; it does not claim globally solved blind inversion.

### Neural-operator formalism and local task map
To make the self-calibration claim precise, we write the degraded optical observation as a state-indexed operator equation rather than as one fixed image-to-image map. Let x belong to an object function space X, let theta belong to a latent degradation space Theta, and let r be a known pilot field. The common-path observation is generated by an operator A_theta acting jointly on x and r, followed by intensity readout y = M(A_theta(x, r)). A passive diffractive processor or downstream reconstruction block is then modeled as a task map G_phi: Y -> Z, where Y is the measurement space and Z is the output space associated with one downstream task. Under this notation, the ordinary no-reference setting uses y0 = M(A_theta(x, 0)), whereas the pilot-assisted setting uses yr = M(A_theta(x, r)). The manuscript's bounded hypothesis is that the pilot changes the observation operator family from a map that marginalizes over theta to a map that reveals part of the current theta-dependence inside the same physical measurement.

The resulting theory claim is local in operator space. Around a nominal operating point (x_*, theta_*), we consider the Fréchet linearization of the observation map and a task loss L(G_phi(y), T(x, theta)) for a task target T. In this neighborhood, the pilot-induced increase in Fisher information and the pilot-induced reduction in posterior covariance can be propagated through the local Jacobian of G_phi to obtain a task-level error-floor comparison. This is the role of Figure 3: not to prove that a fixed diffractive neural operator universally inverts the unknown degradation, but to state that, when the local linearization is valid and the task map is sufficiently regular, a common-path pilot can reduce downstream loss floors by shrinking uncertainty along the task-relevant operator directions.

### Forward models and degradation families
The evidence package uses four forward-model layers. The first is a Gaussian shared-blur surrogate that tests the mechanism in a minimal setting. The second is a wave-optics pupil model with dynamic Zernike aberrations, which serves as the strongest current physically grounded recovery result. The third is a task-level surrogate family used to link pilot-state information to reconstruction, classification residual, and inverse-design proxy losses. The fourth is a phase-only passive diffractive processor protocol that directly compares ordinary D2NN and pilot-assisted D2NN variants. Only the first processor-level prototype is currently available, and it should be treated as partial evidence rather than a definitive device result.

### Metrics, controls, and evidence labeling
Primary current performance metrics are OOD reconstruction PSNR and PSF mismatch, with coefficient-recovery errors used only as secondary diagnostics because they are not uniformly aligned with the strongest recovery trends in the current dataset. Figure 1 compares no-reference, non-common-path, wrong-reference, and common-path conditions at the mechanism level. Figure 2 uses the same contrast to test persistence in the Zernike wave-optics model. Figure 3 reports pilot-information and local task-level analyses. Figure 4 compares reconstruction, classification residual, and inverse-design proxy behavior under the same conditional-observation logic. Figure 5 evaluates the passive-processor protocol. Throughout the manuscript, statements are marked by status: verified, partially verified, or unverified.

## Results
### Result 1. Common-path reference reduces uncertainty in the minimal mechanism model
Figure 1 establishes the conditional-observation mechanism rather than a device claim. In the Gaussian surrogate study, the OOD stronger-aberration set gave a mean reconstruction PSNR of 19.669 dB without a reference and 20.268 dB with a non-common-path reference, whereas the common-path pilot reached 21.990 dB. This ordering is the first direct evidence that a pilot sharing the same instantaneous degradation can improve recovery beyond both no-reference and mismatched-reference baselines. The argument role of Figure 1 is therefore narrow but essential: it shows that the common-path pilot is not merely adding optical power, but can carry condition-specific information relevant to reconstruction.

### Result 2. The same direction persists in a wave-optics model with dynamic Zernike aberrations
Figure 2 moves from surrogate intuition to a more realistic coherent-propagation model. Under the Zernike pupil protocol, the OOD stronger-aberration set produced a mean reconstruction PSNR of 37.069 dB for no-reference, 37.078 dB for non-common-path, and 38.422 dB for common-path input. The common-path setting also achieved the smallest mean PSF mismatch, with a mean PSF MSE of 4.217e-06 compared with 1.279e-05 for no-reference and 1.155e-05 for non-common-path. Figure 2 therefore carries the manuscript's strongest verified recovery result: the common-path advantage survives a wave-optics model in which the degradation originates from dynamic pupil aberrations rather than from a Gaussian surrogate blur.

### Result 3. Information-theoretic and task-level analyses explain why the pilot can help, but only locally
Figure 3 provides the theoretical bridge between the pilot observation and downstream performance. The current CRLB-style scan shows that the pilot observation model carries finite Fisher information over much of the explored train and OOD region, with median trace values of 6.452e-04 and 1.736e-04, respectively. The accompanying tight-bound note links pilot-channel covariance to downstream task loss through a local curvature sandwich. The resulting claim must remain restrained. Figure 3 supports the statement that the pilot can lower local task-level error floors when the observation model and task map satisfy the stated assumptions. It does not prove that a fixed passive processor will always recover the latent degradation or solve a general blind inverse problem.

### Result 4. Cross-domain demonstration is strongest for reconstruction and weaker for inference and design
Figure 4 prevents overgeneralization by showing where the current positive signal is strongest and where it remains modest across three downstream domains that share the same latent degradation but not the same output space. In the current cross-task surrogate evaluation, the common-path condition improves OOD reconstruction PSNR from 34.837 dB to 36.782 dB relative to no-reference. It also lowers the classification true-class residual from 0.035464 to 0.034616 and yields only a very small improvement in inverse-design target MSE, from 0.002317 to 0.002315. This package is best interpreted as a cross-domain demonstration of the same conditional-observation mechanism: the pilot helps most when the downstream map must preserve fine field information for image recovery, helps modestly when the task compresses the observation into a class-discriminative statistic, and becomes nearly neutral when the target is an inverse-design proxy only weakly aligned with the pilot-revealed state coordinates. Figure 4 therefore argues for operator-level task dependence rather than a universal advantage. Reconstruction is the clearest positive regime in the current evidence chain, classification residual shows a mild improvement, and inverse design remains too weak to support a broad claim.

### Result 5. A stable ordinary-versus-common-path processor window exists, but exclusion is only near-threshold under paired diagnostics
Figure 5 is the manuscript's decisive bottleneck figure, and it must still be read conservatively. The first two-layer phase-only reconstruction-only D2NN comparison yielded a neutral-to-negative result, with the common-path condition falling 0.191 dB below the ordinary D2NN. A revised three-layer self-calibrating phase-only prototype then produced a weak positive object-zone OOD signal: the common-path condition reached 11.559 dB, compared with 11.356 dB for the ordinary D2NN, 11.505 dB for the non-common-path reference, and 11.350 dB for the wrong-reference control. Subsequent lightweight local scans sharpened the ordinary-versus-common-path comparison. In the ordinary-focused local window, a one-layer configuration with reference_weight 0.16 and phase_mix 0.15 yields a mean common-minus-ordinary advantage of +0.438 dB, with the worst repeat still at +0.390 dB. The stronger wrong-reference test is now best summarized by the structurally redesigned `sparse_tracker_decoy + occupancy_guarded` branch confirmed in round17 under 14 repeats. In that confirmed window, the common-minus-ordinary mean is +0.638 dB and the worst repeat remains +0.413 dB, while the common-minus-wrong-reference mean is +0.278 dB and the worst repeat remains +0.047 dB. At the paired risk-sample level, the lower-decile margin reaches +0.090 dB and the positive fraction reaches 0.964, showing that structural redesign moves the exclusion test into a mostly positive regime under higher-repeat confirmation. The remaining limitation is that the risk-pair minimum is still -0.025 dB, so exclusion is not yet uniformly positive at the hardest tail. Figure 5 therefore supports a narrower but stronger processor-level statement: the pilot-assisted processor can outperform the ordinary baseline in a repeat-stable local regime, and structural redesign makes the stricter wrong-reference diagnostic mostly positive under higher-repeat confirmation, but one negative tail event remains and the result should not yet be described as fully closed robust exclusion.

## Discussion
Taken together, Figures 1-5 support a coherent but incomplete story. The main verified point is that a common-path pilot can encode useful information about the current degradation realization and improve recovery under the present surrogate and wave-optics protocols. The theoretical analyses in Figure 3 and the cross-domain comparison in Figure 4 help explain why this improvement is strongest for reconstruction-oriented mappings and weaker elsewhere. In the neural-operator language used here, the pilot is useful when it reveals state directions that remain visible after the downstream task projection. This mechanism differs from ordinary robustness training because the pilot is meant to expose the current degradation instance, not merely average over a training distribution.

The same evidence also draws a hard boundary around the manuscript. Figure 5 now shows more than the mere existence of a weak prototype: it shows that the ordinary-versus-common-path comparison can be pushed into a locally repeat-stable positive window in the current lightweight passive protocol, and that structural redesign can make the stricter paired wrong-reference diagnostic positive at the lower-decile level under higher-repeat confirmation. But the exclusion side of the argument is still not fully closed, because one risk-pair tail event remains slightly negative. The current manuscript must therefore present the processor-level result as a stable ordinary-baseline advantage with upgraded but not absolute exclusion evidence, rather than as a mature optical self-calibration demonstration. That restraint is important because the partially negative tail is scientifically informative: it shows that adding a pilot channel and redesigning the wrong-reference structure can stabilize most of the exclusion test before it stabilizes the hardest worst-case instances needed for a stronger claim.

Several failure modes remain open. A pilot with too little information content may not sufficiently constrain the latent state. Reference mismatch or non-common-path corruption can erase the advantage. Stronger digital baselines may yet absorb or outperform the pilot information if trained with richer conditional structure. The processor-level effect may also remain fragile under seed changes, pilot-strength variation, layer-count scaling, turbulence, thin phase-screen scattering, fabrication errors, or detector noise. None of those regimes is yet verified, so they should appear as explicit outstanding risks rather than hidden assumptions.

## Summary
This work advances a bounded conditional-optics hypothesis: a common-path pilot can supply useful information about the instantaneous degradation state in imaging through dynamic aberrations. The strongest verified evidence comes from the wave-optics Zernike protocol, the pilot-information analysis, the reconstruction-dominant cross-task results summarized in Figures 2-4, and a repeat-stable local ordinary-versus-common-path processor window in Figure 5 that now also shows a positive risk-pair lower decile under structurally redesigned wrong-reference confirmation. The decisive remaining gap is no longer the absence of a processor-level positive exclusion direction, but the remaining small negative worst-tail event under the hardest risk-pair statistic.
