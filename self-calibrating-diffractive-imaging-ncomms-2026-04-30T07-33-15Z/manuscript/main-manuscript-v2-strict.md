# A phase-only diffractive frontend for hybrid self-calibrated imaging through dynamic aberrations

## Status and claim boundary

- Target journal line: Nature Communications
- This version is a strict reviewer-facing rewrite aligned to the current evidence package in the active long-term project space.
- The supported claim is not a fully autonomous all-optical reconstruction system.
- The supported claim is that a physically constrained phase-only diffractive frontend, coupled to a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage, improves restoration under dynamic aberrations and retains a modest but positive mean gain under held-out object-family shift.

## Abstract

Dynamic aberrations make coherent imaging a time-varying inverse problem whose state is not directly available at measurement time. We study whether a fixed phase-only diffractive frontend can encode calibration-sensitive information before digital reconstruction. The tested system is hybrid optical-digital: a phase-only diffractive stack is followed by intensity detection and a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage. Under a pre-registered dual-ledger benchmark separating held-out aberration realizations from held-out object families, `phase_only_stack` achieved the largest average gain across the two ledgers, with an aggregate PSNR gain of 4.057 dB (95% CI, 3.931-4.183). The effect was strongly regime-dependent: the gain was large for held-out aberrations and modest under held-out object-family shift, where `reference_psf_deconvolution` remained stronger and `phase_only_stack` retained a positive but smaller gain of 0.521 dB with a better-than-fixed fraction of 0.557. Project-local proxy natural-image stress tests further showed that transfer beyond the synthetic ledgers requires training-distribution alignment. First-order simulated tolerance diagnosis identified phase-mask quantization and lateral displacement as dominant vulnerabilities; robust-mask training mitigated 1 px shift and 3/4-bit quantization, but not larger displacement. These results support a bounded conclusion: a physically constrained diffractive frontend can improve average restoration under dynamic aberrations in a hybrid pipeline, while broader natural-image and hardware claims require stronger validation.

## Introduction

Dynamic aberrations, system drift, and scattering turn coherent imaging into an inverse problem whose forward operator changes from sample to sample, while the instantaneous degradation state is usually unknown at inference time. Existing solutions can compensate for this variability, but they typically rely on active wavefront sensing, adaptive hardware, iterative correction, or substantial digital post-processing. A central unresolved question is therefore whether a fixed optical frontend can encode useful information about the current aberration state before electronic reconstruction.

Diffractive optical processors are attractive in this setting because they can impose strong physical structure at the measurement stage. Yet most diffractive imaging pipelines are optimized for fixed or distribution-averaged forward models rather than for sample-specific optical self-calibration. The relevant test is therefore narrower than full optical autonomy: can a fixed phase-only diffractive frontend improve downstream restoration under a controlled protocol that distinguishes generalization across aberration realizations from generalization across object families?

To answer that question, we use a pre-registered dual-ledger benchmark that separates `same_family_heldout_aberration` from `new_family_heldout_object_family`. This separation is essential because a method can perform strongly on unseen aberrations while still depending heavily on the training object families. The present evidence supports a bounded conclusion. The phase-only frontend gives the strongest overall mean gain in the unified comparison, but the benchmark is strongly asymmetric: performance is large and stable for held-out aberrations, whereas the gain under object-family shift is modest, though still positive on average. The manuscript is therefore written around a constrained claim: the system should be interpreted as a hybrid optical-digital frontend with measurable average benefit under dynamic aberrations, not as a fully optical or uniformly robust reconstructor.

## Methods

### Hybrid phase-only frontend

The system consists of a phase-only diffractive frontend followed by intensity detection and a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage. We therefore report it as a hybrid optical-digital pipeline rather than as all-optical reconstruction. The `phase_only_stack` used in the unified comparison contains 5 diffractive layers with 10 phase-basis coefficients per layer, for 50 optical trainable parameters, followed by a ridge regressor operating on the detected low-resolution features.

### Dual-ledger evaluation protocol

All comparisons followed a fixed dual-ledger protocol. `same_family_heldout_aberration` holds out aberration realizations while preserving the training object-family distribution, whereas `new_family_heldout_object_family` withholds entire object families from training. All methods used the same seeds, aberration schedules, preprocessing, low-resolution target size, and aggregation pipeline. No samples were removed after evaluation, and seed-level and sample-level outputs are retained in the project results directory.

### Compared methods

We compared five methods under the same protocol: `phase_only_stack`, `reference_psf_deconvolution`, `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, `spectral_frontend`, and `trainable_surrogate_ridge`. `reference_psf_deconvolution` uses the measured or simulated reference PSF directly at test time and should therefore be interpreted as a reference-guided classical comparator rather than as a generic baseline. `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, `spectral_frontend`, and `trainable_surrogate_ridge` use the same distorted input and reference channels but differ in feature construction and capacity. Fairness accounting and parameter counts are provided in the Supplementary Information.

### Statistical aggregation

The unified comparison used 10 seeds (`0-9`). For each seed, performance was aggregated within each ledger and then averaged across the two ledgers so that the overall score was not driven by unequal sample counts. The primary statistic in the main text is mean PSNR gain over the fixed baseline; we also report the fraction of evaluated samples for which each method exceeds the fixed baseline.

## Results

### The phase-only frontend provides the best average dual-ledger trade-off under the registered benchmark

We first assessed the hybrid optical-digital system under a pre-registered dual-ledger protocol that separates generalization across aberration realizations from generalization across object families. Under the shared 10-seed benchmark, `phase_only_stack` achieved the largest overall mean PSNR gain over the fixed baseline among the tested methods, with an aggregate gain of 4.057 dB (95% CI, 3.931-4.183). The corresponding gains were 2.706 dB for `reference_psf_deconvolution`, 2.393 dB for `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, 1.891 dB for `spectral_frontend`, and 1.380 dB for `trainable_surrogate_ridge`. The appropriate interpretation is therefore not uniform superiority across regimes, but the best average dual-ledger trade-off under the registered aggregation rule. Relative to the parameter-matched digital surrogate, the hybrid method retains a clear advantage on the held-out aberration ledger while only a narrow margin remains on the held-out object-family ledger.

This overall advantage should not be interpreted as uniform dominance across all robustness metrics. The better-than-fixed fraction of `phase_only_stack` was 0.7786, whereas `reference_psf_deconvolution` reached 1.0. The present evidence therefore supports a narrower conclusion: the phase-only frontend yields the strongest average trade-off in the registered benchmark, but it still exhibits a non-negligible negative tail that must be accounted for explicitly.

### The gain is large for held-out aberrations but modest for held-out object-family shift

The ledger-resolved analysis shows a clear asymmetry between the two generalization regimes. On `same_family_heldout_aberration`, `phase_only_stack` achieved a mean PSNR gain of 7.593 dB with a better-than-fixed fraction of 1.0. By contrast, on `new_family_heldout_object_family`, the gain decreased to 0.521 dB, with a better-than-fixed fraction of 0.5573. The main difficulty of the current system is therefore not aberration-only generalization, but transfer across previously unseen object families.

This distinction is important for claim calibration. The positive mean gain on `new_family_heldout_object_family` argues against a purely memorization-based explanation, yet the effect size is substantially smaller than in the same-family ledger. A reviewer-safe interpretation is therefore that the phase-only frontend retains a modest average benefit under object-family shift, rather than that it has already solved robust object-level generalization.

### The strongest baseline remains competitive in the harder shift regime

The object-family ledger also clarifies where the present method is and is not strongest. Although `phase_only_stack` ranks first in the overall benchmark, it is not the best method on every sub-regime. In particular, `reference_psf_deconvolution`, the reference-guided classical comparator, achieved a larger mean PSNR gain than `phase_only_stack` on `new_family_heldout_object_family` (2.511 dB versus 0.521 dB), while `trainable_surrogate_ridge` became slightly negative on average (-0.1387 dB). This pattern narrows the main conclusion: the current phase-only frontend provides the best average dual-ledger trade-off, but explicit test-time reference information remains more effective in the hardest object-family-shift regime.

### Proxy natural-image stress tests reveal a training-distribution dependence rather than a complete failure of the optical frontend

To probe realism beyond the synthetic ledgers, we next evaluated the pipeline on project-local proxy natural-image subsets matched to the frozen `ImageNet-1k` and `COCO` protocols. These runs must be interpreted strictly as proxy natural-image stress tests and not as official `ImageNet` or `COCO` results. Under the original synthetic-only training regime, the pipeline failed strongly on both proxy datasets, with mean PSNR gains over fixed of -10.3924 dB for `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and -12.8547 dB for `COCO` proxy images. Thus, the initial model did not transfer to natural-image statistics in any meaningful way.

After retraining under a mixed regime that incorporated proxy natural images into the training distribution, the sign of the effect reversed. In a first rerun, the mean PSNR gain over fixed reached +0.7458 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and +1.3334 dB on `COCO` proxy images. We then thickened this evaluation to 5 seeds with 12 held-out cases per seed. The resulting mean gains remained positive at +0.9841 +/- 0.2501 dB for `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and +1.4520 +/- 0.1370 dB for `COCO` proxy images (95% CI half-width), with mean better-than-fixed fractions of 0.7736 and 0.9111, respectively. These data support a narrower inference than broad natural-image generalization: the negative transfer seen in the original model is largely attributable to training-distribution mismatch, and a positive effect can be recovered when the training distribution is partially aligned with natural-image structure.

### Common perturbations are tolerated, whereas mask-specific engineering errors dominate the current robustness risk

We next examined tolerance in the mixed synthetic plus proxy-natural regime. Across common perturbations, the phase-only frontend remained positive on both proxy-natural datasets. For propagation-distance error within +/-5% z, `phase_only_stack` stayed positive across all tested levels, for example from +1.0587 dB to +1.0733 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and from +1.4539 dB to +1.4575 dB on `COCO` proxy images. Under reference-channel intensity noise, the method also remained positive, with gains of +1.0432 dB and +0.9048 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images at noise levels 0.01 and 0.02, and +1.3594 dB and +1.1405 dB on `COCO` proxy images at the same noise levels. These results indicate that mild common perturbations do not eliminate the recovered proxy-natural advantage.

The dominant robustness liability instead arises from phase-mask-specific engineering perturbations. In the initial tolerance pass, 1 px and 2 px lateral shift, as well as 3-bit and 4-bit quantization, drove `phase_only_stack` strongly negative on both proxy-natural datasets. For example, 1 px shift reduced the mean PSNR gain to -10.8126 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and -13.7673 dB on `COCO` proxy images, whereas 3-bit quantization yielded -10.5161 dB and -13.0224 dB, respectively. The main reviewer-facing hardware concern is therefore not mild shared-input perturbation, but the sensitivity of the learned mask to fabrication and alignment errors.

### Robust-mask training repairs first-order quantization and alignment failures but not larger displacement

To test whether this engineering sensitivity is itself learnable, we performed a robust-mask mitigation pass in which the phase-only model was trained with in-loop exposure to unperturbed masks, 4-bit quantization, 3-bit quantization, and 1 px lateral shift. This intervention improved clean-reference performance to +2.8116 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and +2.1783 dB on `COCO` proxy images, compared with tolerance-baseline gains of +1.0733 dB and +1.4540 dB, respectively.

More importantly, the same mitigation converted previously catastrophic first-order engineering failures into positive-gain regimes. Under 1 px lateral shift, the mean PSNR gain became +2.3911 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and +2.0569 dB on `COCO` proxy images. Under 3-bit quantization, the gains became +2.3374 dB and +2.0673 dB, and under 4-bit quantization they became +2.5652 dB and +2.1784 dB, respectively. Nevertheless, 2 px lateral shift remained strongly negative, with gains of -11.1708 dB on `ImageNet-1k` proxy images and -13.9748 dB on `COCO` proxy images. The mitigation therefore repairs the dominant first-order fabrication and alignment failures, but it does not yet establish broad hardware robustness.

## Discussion

Taken together, the data support a narrow but coherent conclusion. In the registered dual-ledger benchmark, the phase-only diffractive frontend provides the strongest overall mean gain among the tested methods, but that advantage is carried mainly by the held-out-aberration ledger. The object-family-shift ledger remains substantially harder, and the manuscript should treat its positive mean effect as modest rather than definitive. This boundary is central to the paper's credibility: the present result is a hybrid optical-digital gain under dynamic aberrations, not a claim of fully optical autonomy or universal robustness.

The proxy natural-image and tolerance analyses sharpen the same point. The strongly negative synthetic-only transfer shows that the original model does not automatically generalize to natural-image structure, whereas the recovery of positive gains after mixed training indicates that the optical frontend benefits from closer alignment between training and deployment distributions. Likewise, mild shared perturbations do not erase the recovered gain, but phase-mask quantization and lateral shift remain serious engineering stressors. Robust-mask training repairs the dominant first-order failures, yet the persistence of the 2 px shift failure mode means that the current evidence should be framed as first-order simulated tolerance diagnosis rather than as hardware robustness.

## Limitations

Several limitations should remain explicit. First, the present implementation is a hybrid optical-digital pipeline, because the optical frontend is followed by a digital reconstruction stage. Second, the strongest generalization result lies in the held-out-aberration ledger rather than the held-out-object-family ledger. Third, the natural-image analyses are based on project-local proxy subsets and must not be presented as official `ImageNet-1k` or `COCO` benchmark results. Fourth, the tolerance evidence now covers the main first-order perturbation branches, but the executed robustness package still supports only a constrained engineering conclusion, especially because 2 px lateral shift remains a clear failure mode.

## Conclusion

The current evidence package supports a restrained Nature Communications-level conclusion: a phase-only diffractive optical frontend can improve average image restoration under dynamic aberrations within a hybrid optical-digital pipeline, and this benefit survives both a registered object-family-shift benchmark and a project-local proxy natural-image stress test, albeit with much smaller effect sizes than those observed for held-out aberration generalization. The same data also show that the present system is limited by training-distribution dependence and by mask-specific engineering sensitivity, while robust-mask training can partially repair the dominant first-order failures. The most defensible claim is therefore not that self-calibrated optical imaging has been solved in full, but that a physically constrained diffractive frontend can deliver measurable average benefit under dynamic aberrations when its claim boundary is defined carefully.
