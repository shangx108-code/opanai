# A phase-only diffractive frontend for hybrid self-calibrated imaging through dynamic aberrations

## Status and claim boundary

- Target journal line: Nature Communications
- This version is a strict reviewer-facing rewrite aligned to the current evidence package in the active long-term project space.
- The supported claim is not a fully autonomous all-optical reconstruction system.
- The supported claim is that a physically constrained phase-only diffractive frontend, coupled to a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage, improves restoration under dynamic aberrations and retains a modest but positive mean gain under held-out object-family shift.

## Abstract

Dynamic aberrations make coherent imaging a time-varying inverse problem whose state is not directly available at measurement time. We study whether a fixed phase-only diffractive frontend can encode calibration-sensitive information before digital reconstruction. The tested system is hybrid optical-digital: a phase-only diffractive stack is followed by intensity detection and a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage. Under a pre-registered dual-ledger benchmark separating held-out aberration realizations from held-out object families, `phase_only_stack` achieved the largest average gain across the two ledgers, with an aggregate PSNR gain of 4.057 dB (95% CI, 3.931-4.183). The effect was strongly regime-dependent: the gain was large for held-out aberrations and modest under held-out object-family shift, where `reference_psf_deconvolution` remained stronger and `phase_only_stack` retained a positive but smaller gain of 0.521 dB with a better-than-fixed fraction of 0.557. A public-open-data natural-image rerun on frozen `Kodak-PCD0992` and `UCID-1338` subsets showed that transfer beyond the synthetic ledgers is strongly dataset-dependent: mixed training reduces the failure sharply, but `UCID` becomes positive whereas `Kodak-PCD0992` remains mildly negative. First-order simulated tolerance diagnosis identified phase-mask quantization and lateral displacement as dominant vulnerabilities; robust-mask training raised the clean-margin performance and repaired several first-order perturbation cases, but linked scans over phase noise, layer misalignment, and wavelength drift showed that this mitigation did not widen the tolerance window beyond the clean `UCID` branch. These results support a bounded conclusion: a physically constrained diffractive frontend can improve average restoration under dynamic aberrations in a hybrid pipeline, while broader natural-image and hardware claims require strong claim-boundary control.

## Introduction

Dynamic aberrations, system drift, and scattering turn coherent imaging into an inverse problem whose forward operator changes from sample to sample, while the instantaneous degradation state is usually unknown at inference time. Existing solutions can compensate for this variability, but they typically rely on active wavefront sensing, adaptive hardware, iterative correction, or substantial digital post-processing. A central unresolved question is therefore whether a fixed optical frontend can encode useful information about the current aberration state before electronic reconstruction.

Diffractive optical processors are attractive in this setting because they can impose strong physical structure at the measurement stage. Yet most diffractive imaging pipelines are optimized for fixed or distribution-averaged forward models rather than for sample-specific optical self-calibration. The relevant test is therefore narrower than full optical autonomy: can a fixed phase-only diffractive frontend improve downstream restoration under a controlled protocol that distinguishes generalization across aberration realizations from generalization across object families?

To answer that question, we use a pre-registered dual-ledger benchmark that separates `same_family_heldout_aberration` from `new_family_heldout_object_family`. This separation is essential because a method can perform strongly on unseen aberrations while still depending heavily on the training object families. The present evidence supports a bounded conclusion. The phase-only frontend gives the strongest overall mean gain in the unified comparison, but the benchmark is strongly asymmetric: performance is large and stable for held-out aberrations, whereas the gain under object-family shift is modest, though still positive on average. The manuscript is therefore written around a constrained claim: the system should be interpreted as a hybrid optical-digital frontend with measurable average benefit under dynamic aberrations, not as a fully optical or uniformly robust reconstructor.

## Methods

### Hybrid phase-only frontend

The system consists of a phase-only diffractive frontend followed by intensity detection and a fixed-capacity digital ridge reconstruction stage. We therefore report it as a hybrid optical-digital pipeline rather than as all-optical reconstruction. The `phase_only_stack` used in the unified comparison contains 5 diffractive layers with 10 phase-basis coefficients per layer, for 50 optical trainable parameters, followed by a ridge regressor operating on the detected low-resolution features.

### Dual-ledger evaluation protocol

All comparisons followed a fixed dual-ledger protocol. `same_family_heldout_aberration` holds out aberration realizations while preserving the training object-family distribution, whereas `new_family_heldout_object_family` withholds entire object families from training. All methods used the same seeds, aberration schedules, preprocessing, low-resolution target size, and aggregation pipeline. No samples were removed after evaluation, and seed-level and sample-level outputs are retained in the project results directory.

### Compared methods

We compared five methods under the same protocol: `phase_only_stack`, `reference_psf_deconvolution`, `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, `spectral_frontend`, and `trainable_surrogate_ridge`. `reference_psf_deconvolution` uses the measured or simulated reference PSF directly at test time and should therefore be interpreted as a reference-guided classical comparator rather than as a generic baseline. `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, `spectral_frontend`, and `trainable_surrogate_ridge` use the same distorted input and reference channels but differ in feature construction and capacity. Fairness accounting and parameter counts are provided in the Supplementary Information.

### Statistical aggregation

The unified comparison used 10 seeds (`0-9`). For each seed, performance was aggregated within each ledger and then averaged across the two ledgers so that the overall score was not driven by unequal sample counts. The primary statistic in the main text is mean PSNR gain over the fixed baseline; we also report the fraction of evaluated samples for which each method exceeds the fixed baseline.

## Results

### The phase-only frontend provides the best average dual-ledger trade-off under the registered benchmark

We first assessed the hybrid optical-digital system under a pre-registered dual-ledger protocol that separates generalization across aberration realizations from generalization across object families. Under the shared 10-seed benchmark, `phase_only_stack` achieved the largest overall mean PSNR gain over the fixed baseline among the tested methods, with an aggregate gain of 4.057 dB (95% CI, 3.931-4.183). The corresponding gains were 2.706 dB for `reference_psf_deconvolution`, 2.393 dB for `parameter_matched_digital_surrogate`, 1.891 dB for `spectral_frontend`, and 1.380 dB for `trainable_surrogate_ridge`. The appropriate interpretation is therefore not uniform superiority across regimes, but the best average dual-ledger trade-off under the registered aggregation rule. Relative to the parameter-matched digital surrogate, the hybrid method retains a clear advantage on the held-out aberration ledger while only a narrow margin remains on the held-out object-family ledger.

This overall advantage should not be interpreted as uniform dominance across all robustness metrics. The better-than-fixed fraction of `phase_only_stack` was 0.7786, whereas `reference_psf_deconvolution` reached 1.0. The present evidence therefore supports a narrower conclusion: the phase-only frontend yields the strongest average trade-off in the registered benchmark, but it still exhibits a non-negligible negative tail that must be accounted for explicitly.

### The gain is large for held-out aberrations but modest for held-out object-family shift

The ledger-resolved analysis shows a clear asymmetry between the two generalization regimes. On `same_family_heldout_aberration`, `phase_only_stack` achieved a mean PSNR gain of 7.593 dB with a better-than-fixed fraction of 1.0. By contrast, on `new_family_heldout_object_family`, the gain decreased to 0.521 dB, with a better-than-fixed fraction of 0.5573. The main difficulty of the current system is therefore not aberration-only generalization, but transfer across previously unseen object families.

This distinction is important for claim calibration. The positive mean gain on `new_family_heldout_object_family` argues against a purely memorization-based explanation, yet the effect size is substantially smaller than in the same-family ledger. A reviewer-safe interpretation is therefore that the phase-only frontend retains a modest average benefit under object-family shift, rather than that it has already solved robust object-level generalization.

### The strongest baseline remains competitive in the harder shift regime

The object-family ledger also clarifies where the present method is and is not strongest. Although `phase_only_stack` ranks first in the overall benchmark, it is not the best method on every sub-regime. In particular, `reference_psf_deconvolution`, the reference-guided classical comparator, achieved a larger mean PSNR gain than `phase_only_stack` on `new_family_heldout_object_family` (2.511 dB versus 0.521 dB), while `trainable_surrogate_ridge` became slightly negative on average (-0.1387 dB). This pattern narrows the main conclusion: the current phase-only frontend provides the best average dual-ledger trade-off, but explicit test-time reference information remains more effective in the hardest object-family-shift regime.

### Public Kodak/UCID natural-image evaluation reveals strong dataset dependence

To probe realism beyond the synthetic ledgers, we next evaluated the pipeline on staged public-open-data subsets from `Kodak-PCD0992` and `UCID-1338`. These runs are real public-data evaluations with frozen manifests, but they are not benchmark-root `ImageNet-1k` or `COCO` results. Under the original synthetic-only training regime, the pipeline failed strongly on both public datasets, with mean PSNR gains over fixed of -16.1404 dB for `Kodak-PCD0992` and -12.1478 dB for `UCID`. Thus, the initial model did not transfer to natural-image statistics in any meaningful way.

After retraining under a mixed regime that incorporated public natural images into the training distribution, the failure was reduced sharply but not uniformly repaired. In a first rerun, the mean PSNR gain over fixed reached -1.1311 dB on `Kodak-PCD0992` and +1.3828 dB on `UCID`. We then thickened this evaluation to 5 seeds with 12 held-out cases per seed. The resulting mean gains remained at -1.0544 +/- 0.1434 dB for `Kodak-PCD0992` and +1.7261 +/- 0.2319 dB for `UCID` (95% CI half-width), with mean better-than-fixed fractions of 0.1403 and 0.8153, respectively. These data support a narrower inference than broad natural-image generalization: the negative transfer seen in the original model is largely attributable to training-distribution mismatch, but the mixed-training recovery is strongly dataset-dependent rather than universally positive.

### Common perturbations are dataset-dependent, whereas mask-specific engineering errors dominate the current robustness risk

We next examined tolerance in the mixed synthetic plus public-natural regime. Across common perturbations, the phase-only frontend remained positive on `UCID` but negative on `Kodak-PCD0992`, even at clean reference and under propagation-distance error within +/-5% z. At clean reference, `phase_only_stack` yielded +1.6409 dB on `UCID` but -1.1084 dB on `Kodak-PCD0992`; under +/-5% propagation-distance error, the `UCID` gain stayed near +1.64 dB whereas the `Kodak-PCD0992` gain stayed near -1.11 dB. Under reference-channel intensity noise of 0.01 and 0.02, `UCID` remained positive at +1.6014 dB and +1.4643 dB, while `Kodak-PCD0992` remained negative at -1.1789 dB and -1.3759 dB. These results indicate that mild shared perturbations do not erase the public-data gain where it exists, but they also do not rescue the harder `Kodak-PCD0992` subset.

The dominant robustness liability instead arises from phase-mask-specific engineering perturbations. In the initial tolerance pass, 1 px and 2 px lateral shift, as well as 3-bit and 4-bit quantization, drove `phase_only_stack` strongly negative on both public datasets. For example, 1 px shift reduced the mean PSNR gain to -15.6732 dB on `Kodak-PCD0992` and -11.4353 dB on `UCID`, whereas 3-bit quantization yielded -13.9654 dB and -9.6618 dB, respectively. The main reviewer-facing hardware concern is therefore not mild shared-input perturbation, but the sensitivity of the learned mask to fabrication and alignment errors.

### Robust-mask training raises clean margin and repairs several first-order failures, but does not widen the linked tolerance window

To test whether this engineering sensitivity is itself learnable, we performed a robust-mask mitigation pass in which the phase-only model was trained with in-loop exposure to unperturbed masks, 4-bit quantization, 3-bit quantization, and 1 px lateral shift. This intervention improved clean-reference performance to +2.5993 dB on `UCID` and to -0.2182 dB on `Kodak-PCD0992`, compared with tolerance-baseline gains of +1.6409 dB and -1.1084 dB, respectively. In that limited sense, the mitigation raises the clean operating margin on the one public dataset that is already positive and narrows the clean-point deficit on the harder Kodak subset.

More importantly, the same mitigation largely repaired the first-order engineering failures, but in a strongly asymmetric way. On `UCID`, the mean PSNR gain became +2.3329 dB under 1 px lateral shift, +2.4140 dB under 3-bit quantization, and +2.5972 dB under 4-bit quantization. On `Kodak-PCD0992`, the corresponding gains improved dramatically from large negative values to only slightly negative values: -0.3333 dB under 1 px shift, -0.3274 dB under 3-bit quantization, and -0.2536 dB under 4-bit quantization. Nevertheless, 2 px lateral shift remained strongly negative on both datasets, with gains of -16.5628 dB on `Kodak-PCD0992` and -12.3464 dB on `UCID`.

We then stress-tested the same robust-mask model under a linked fabrication-style scan that jointly varied phase noise, layer misalignment, and wavelength drift. This scan showed that the clean `UCID` branch remains positive through `2%` wavelength drift, improving from +1.6409 dB in the unmitigated model to +2.5993 dB at `0%` drift and to +2.5192 dB at `2%` drift after robust-mask training. However, the pass region did not extend beyond that clean branch: on `UCID`, adding only `0.05 rad` phase noise reduced the mean gain to -4.3309 dB, and adding only mild misalignment (`0.25 px` shift sigma and `0.5 deg` rotation sigma) reduced it to -7.4303 dB. `Kodak-PCD0992` also remained negative at the clean point (`-0.2182 dB`). The mitigation should therefore be described as raising clean margin and repairing selected first-order perturbation cases, not as widening the linked fabrication-tolerance window.

## Discussion

Taken together, the data support a narrow but coherent conclusion. In the registered dual-ledger benchmark, the phase-only diffractive frontend provides the strongest overall mean gain among the tested methods, but that advantage is carried mainly by the held-out-aberration ledger. The object-family-shift ledger remains substantially harder, and the manuscript should treat its positive mean effect as modest rather than definitive. This boundary is central to the paper's credibility: the present result is a hybrid optical-digital gain under dynamic aberrations, not a claim of fully optical autonomy or universal robustness.

The public Kodak/UCID natural-image and tolerance analyses sharpen the same point. The strongly negative synthetic-only transfer shows that the original model does not automatically generalize to natural-image structure, whereas mixed training shows that part of the failure is a distribution-alignment problem rather than a total incompatibility with natural images. At the same time, the public-data rerun makes the claim boundary stricter than before: `UCID` becomes reproducibly positive, but `Kodak-PCD0992` remains mildly negative, so the appropriate conclusion is dataset-dependent partial recovery rather than broad natural-image generalization. Likewise, mild shared perturbations do not erase the `UCID` gain, but phase-mask quantization and lateral shift remain serious engineering stressors. Robust-mask training repairs the dominant first-order failures on `UCID` and sharply improves `Kodak-PCD0992` at the clean point, yet the linked phase-noise-plus-misalignment-plus-wavelength scan shows that the same strategy does not widen the tolerance window beyond the clean `UCID` branch. The current evidence should therefore still be framed as constrained simulated tolerance diagnosis rather than as hardware robustness.

## Limitations

Several limitations should remain explicit. First, the present implementation is a hybrid optical-digital pipeline, because the optical frontend is followed by a digital reconstruction stage. Second, the strongest generalization result lies in the held-out-aberration ledger rather than the held-out-object-family ledger. Third, the natural-image analyses now rest on staged public Kodak/UCID subsets rather than on benchmark-root `ImageNet-1k` or `COCO`, and even within this real public-data branch the outcomes are strongly dataset-dependent. Fourth, the tolerance evidence now includes both first-order and linked fabrication-style scans, but the executed robustness package still supports only a constrained engineering conclusion, especially because `Kodak-PCD0992` remains negative at the clean point after robust-mask training and the linked pass region does not extend beyond clean `UCID`.

## Conclusion

The current evidence package supports a restrained Nature Communications-level conclusion: a phase-only diffractive optical frontend can improve average image restoration under dynamic aberrations within a hybrid optical-digital pipeline, and this benefit survives a registered object-family-shift benchmark while extending only selectively to real public natural-image data. The same data show that natural-image transfer is strongly dataset-dependent and that mask-specific engineering sensitivity remains a central limitation. Robust-mask training can raise clean operating margin and strongly repair several first-order failures on `UCID`, but the linked fabrication-style scan shows that this strategy does not widen the tolerance window beyond the clean `UCID` branch. The most defensible claim is therefore not that self-calibrated optical imaging has been solved in full, but that a physically constrained diffractive frontend can deliver measurable average benefit under dynamic aberrations when its claim boundary is defined carefully and its public-data heterogeneity is stated plainly.
