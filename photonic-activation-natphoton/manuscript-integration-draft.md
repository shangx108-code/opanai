## Introduction block
Photonic neural networks are compelling because optics can execute linear transformations with exceptional bandwidth and potentially low energy. The unresolved cost centre is nonlinear activation: in most architectures it still requires electronic conversion, strong material response or measurement overhead, and the field lacks a common way to judge when those costs are justified. Recent progress has narrowed the gap between concept and hardware. Integrated coherent processors now combine optical matrix algebra, on-chip nonlinear activation and in situ training on a single chip; scattering-based systems show that repeated linear propagation can synthesize nonlinear processing at low power; and field-programmable nonlinear photonic microprocessors demonstrate dynamically reconfigurable polynomial networks. These advances establish that photonic nonlinearity is possible, but they do not supply a common physical accounting of nonlinear activation cost across distinct routes, nor do they determine when paying that cost changes task-level design decisions under detector inefficiency, loss and finite-shot sampling. Here we formulate nonlinear activation as a quantum-resource problem, place lower bounds and implementable measurement-induced routes on the same axes, and then test whether their neuron-level ordering survives when the total activation photon budget is fixed at task level.

## Figure 3 Results block
Figure 3 compares a lower-bound-calibrated activation frontier with two implementable measurement-induced routes on common discrimination-cost axes. This comparison is not a hardware-superiority claim. Its role is to reveal which penalties are universal and which are architecture specific. The dominant global penalty is detector inefficiency, which lifts all implementable routes above the lower bound approximately through the expected `1 / eta` scaling. Measurement architecture then adds a further constant-factor overhead above that detector-limited floor. In the present benchmark, displaced on-off counting often stays closer to the bound than the homodyne-conditioned route, but the gap is regime dependent rather than universal. The main design conclusion is therefore bounded: measurement-induced activation approaches the practical frontier only inside explicitly quantified detector- and sampling-limited regimes.

## Figure 4 Results block
The task-level benchmark in Fig. 4 asks whether Fig. 3 is merely a neuron-level curiosity or whether it changes system-level design. We enforce a fixed total activation photon budget per inference and compare two implementable activation routes on two small nonlinear classification tasks. The answer is mixed. For concentric circles, where the linear baseline is weak, paying for activation is worthwhile throughout the scanned detector-efficiency and budget range. For two moons, where the linear baseline is already strong, the best implementable activation does not outperform the linear model in the scanned range even when the single-neuron frontier leaves residual headroom. The route ranking is also reordered at task level: closeness to the single-neuron bound does not by itself determine the preferred design once width allocation and activation reliability compete for the same photon budget. This mixed result is the manuscript's key systems statement because it converts the activation frontier into a design rule rather than an advocacy claim.

## Discussion block
Taken together, Figs. 3 and 4 separate three questions that are often conflated in photonic-AI discussions: whether a nonlinear response can be realized, how far above the physical frontier a given realization sits, and whether paying for that response is worthwhile for the target task. Recent photonic hardware advances answer the first question increasingly well. The present framework addresses the second and third. It shows that low-photon measurement-induced activation is not universally optimal, but also that dismissing it on architecture grounds alone is too crude: the relevant decision depends on detector efficiency, finite-shot overhead, task geometry and the strength of the linear baseline.
